About

I am an engineer-turned-researcher, conducting research at the intersection of HCI/CSCW and the future of work using qualitative and participatory research and design methods. I take a worker-centered approach to investigating and designing digital labor platforms to support workers' wellbeing—physical, psychological, social, and financial—by situating the concept of wellbeing in an ecological context as individual wellbeing is inseparable from the wellbeing of the environment. Through my research, I aim to foster a more participatory, just, collective, sustainable, and inclusive future(s) of work, irrespective of one's identity-based factors. My research draws on the degrowth/post-growth philosophy to find ways to reduce workers' dependency on the unsustainable growth economy. My research asks:

  • How might we (re)frame work/labor towards nurturing workers’ wellbeing while reducing their dependencies on markets/capitalist corporations?
  • How might we design digital labor platforms to nurture workers’ wellbeing by centering it around environmental sustainability and ecological wellbeing?
My PhD is currently supported by the Brook Byers Institute for Sustainable Systems Graduate Fellowship.

My ways of thinking, doing, and being have been largely shaped by my experiences of living in a conservative family of traditional farmers and navigating sociocultural boundaries as a queer person of color (South Asian) in a region that has been suffering from territorial dispute between India and Pakistan since 1947. I grew up in Jammu and Kashmir, a region prone to terrorism. I moved to Chandigarh for my Bachelor's in Computer Science and Engineering. Then, I moved to Bangalore, where I worked as a Software Engineer for about 2 years, before pursuing higher studies. I graduated from UC Irvine in 2019 with a Master's in Information and Computer Science. Under the guidance of Dr. Bonnie Nardi, for master’s thesis, I focused on understanding the use of social media in raising public awareness and supporting participation in addressing crises of water scarcity and water pollution in Bangalore, India. Growing up and living in diverse contexts and cultures have provided me with various forms of cultural capital, particularly aspirational, linguistic, and resistant capital, that has heped me approach reality from a pluralistic perspective.
